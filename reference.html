The 8 steps of your pitch

①Greet
Zǎoshang hǎo. Lǎoshī hǎo. Dàjiā hǎo. Wǒ jiào ___.
Good morning. Hello teacher. Hello everyone. My name is ___.

Repeat steps ② – ⑦ TWICE — once for each family member.

②Identify
Zhè shì wǒ de ___.
This is my ___. (bàba / māma / gēge / gǒu / māo …)
③Name
Wǒ de ___ jiào ___.
My ___ is called ___.
④Age ⚠ no shì!
Wǒ de ___ ___ suì.
My ___ is ___ years old. (Never: Wǒ de bàba shì 45 suì)
⑤Nationality ⚠ rén required!
Tā shì ___ rén.
He / She is ___ (Chinese / Australian / American …). Never: Tā shì Àodàlìyà.
⑥Description
Wǒ de ___ hěn ___, [hé / dànshì] hěn ___.
1 appearance + 1 personality. Link with hé (and) or dànshì (but). Always say hěn before the adjective!
⑦Location
Tā zhù zài ___.
He / She lives in ___ (Xīní / Běijīng / Shànghǎi …).
⑧Sign off
Xièxie. Zàijiàn!
Thank you. Goodbye!

The 6 deadly errors — memorise!

  1. No shì with age — say ___ suì, never shì ___ suì.
  2. No shì before adjectives — use hěn instead.
  3. Always end nationality with rén (人).
  4. Word order: hěn FIRST, then adjective. Never gāo hěn.
  5. Location needs zhù zài — never just zhù.
  6. No de between hěn and the adjective. Just hěn + adj.

Grammar in detail

Why hěn? — the rule behind step ⑥

In English we say "My dad is clever." The verb "is" connects the person to the adjective. Chinese does NOT do this. Chinese adjectives are already verb-like — they mean "is/are tall," "is/are clever" all by themselves. So we never say shì before an adjective.

But there's a catch: if you just say "Wǒ de bàba cōngming", a Chinese listener hears half a sentence — like "My dad is cleverer…" and waits for the comparison ("…than my brother"). To stop that, we put hěn in front. Hěn here is a marker — it does NOT really mean "very." Treat it as the glue that turns "clever" into a finished sentence.

So the pattern is fixed: Subject + hěn + adjective. Always in that order. Never "gāo hěn". Never "shì hěn gāo". Never "hěn de gāo".

shì vs hěn — when to use which

shì (是) — for NOUNS only hěn (很) — for ADJECTIVES
✓ Tā shì lǎoshī. (He is a teacher.) ✓ Tā hěn cōngming. (He is clever.)
✓ Tā shì Àodàlìyà rén. (He is Australian.) ✓ Tā hěn gāo. (He is tall.)
✓ Wǒ shì xuéshēng. (I am a student.) ✓ Wǒ hěn kuàilè. (I am happy.)

hé (and) — same flavour

Use hé to join two qualities of the same flavour — both nice, both physical, both negative. Think "addition."

  • ✓ Wǒ de bàba hěn gāo hé hěn shuài. (Tall AND handsome — both physical, both positive.)
  • ✓ Wǒ de gǒu hěn xiǎo hé hěn kě'ài. (Small AND cute — both adorable.)
  • ✓ Wǒ de mèimei hěn cōngming hé hěn yǒuhǎo. (Clever AND friendly — both positive personality.)

dànshì (but) — different flavour

Use dànshì to join two contrasting qualities — one positive and one negative, or two clearly different types. Think "however."

  • ✓ Wǒ de gēge hěn gāo dànshì hěn ānjìng. (Tall BUT quiet — physical vs. personality.)
  • ✓ Wǒ de māma hěn piàoliang dànshì hěn yánlì. (Pretty BUT strict — soft vs. tough.)
  • ✓ Wǒ de māo hěn kě'ài dànshì hěn pàng. (Cute BUT chubby — flattering vs. teasing.)

⚠ Non-negotiable: repeat hěn before the second adjective!

✓ hěn gāo hé hěn shuài

✗ hěn gāo hé shuài   ✗ hěn gāo dànshì shuài

👉 Decision rule (1 line)

Same flavour? → hé.   Different flavour? → dànshì.
